The Last Letter

April 8, 2025 By Valvet Dreams Fiction

Part One: The Discovery

Emma Matthews stood in the darkness of her grandmother's attic, motes of dust dancing in the thin beam of sunlight that streamed in through the small dormer window. The air hung thick with memories and the peculiar mustiness that was characteristic of rooms not seen for many years. Emma had never set foot in this corner of her grandmother's Victorian home at thirty-two, but since Grandma Lillian passed away, the task of cleaning out the house rested on her shoulders.

She wiped sweat from her brow with the palm of her hand and gazed out at the mountains of boxes, trunks, and junk furniture that littered her path to the small living room. Three days as an employee, and she'd made barely a dent. Her grandmother had been a collector—a hoarder, others might say—piling up apparently everything that had ever crossed her path in her eighty-seven years.

"I should have employed someone," Emma told herself, tucking a dark lock of hair behind her ear. But she couldn't. These were her grandmother's things, her memories. It didn't feel right to have strangers going through them.

Emma moved toward a beautifully carved wooden chest concealed under the eaves. In contrast to all the rest up in the attic, this one appeared intentionally left there and not carelessly thrown together for storage. It's dark mahogany finish was also dust-free, which indicated it had been taken out more recently than the other treasures around it.

Kneeling beside it, Emma placed her hands on the vine and rose designs on its lid. She knew it was her grandmother's hope chest—the one she had frequently spoken of but never shown anyone, not even Emma's own mother, Catherine.

"What were you keeping secret, Grandma?" Emma whispered as she slowly lifted the heavy lid.

The chest had the things that it should have—hand-stitched linens, a christening dress likely belonging to Emma's mother, sun-wilted photos in paper folders. But when she unfolded a painstakingly folded wedding veil, her fingers encountered something hard beneath the folds of tissue. Pushing aside the diaphanous material, Emma discovered a small wooden box, roughly the size of a paperback book.

Unlike the hope chest, this was a plain box, only having a small brass latch to ornament it. Emma opened it very slowly to see a collection of yellowed envelopes bound together by a faded blue ribbon. They were all but one not sealed, their insides clearly read and returned to their envelopes. But the last envelope was sealed, the word "Elizabeth" in lovely writing across its face.

Elizabeth. Emma furrowed her brow, trying to place the name within her family tree. She recalled that her grandmother had a younger sister named Margaret who passed away in childhood, but no Elizabeth. She gingerly unwrapped the bow and began to read the open letters. They were love letters, written in 1952, and signed with a plain "J."

Heart pounding with excitement of discovery, Emma opened the sealed letter, the last in the pile. Paper thin in her fingers, she paused, whether she ought or not to open something sealed for over seventy years. But curiosity was too strong. Her fingers were feather-light as she carefully broke the seal and opened the letter out inside.

June 15, 1952

My dearest Elizabeth,

Before you can read these words, I will no longer be. Not through my choice, never through choice when it involves departing from you. I was recalled to London too early. My father's illness worsened, and being his only son, I must return to fulfill my responsibilities.

I realize we have spoken of marriage, of living together in your New England you love so well, but destiny has played us false. I could not ask you to leave everything you have and know to travel with me into the uncertain future in a country still healing from war.

What I have omitted—what I could not do in my honored time with you—is that with my return to England are attached obligations which I am unable to evade. My father has spoken for my bride, a match which will bind the destiny of our household and the livelihoods of hundreds whose fate hangs from our factories.

I write this with a breaking heart, knowing that I am a coward for fearing to tell you in your face. But to look into your pain-stained eyes would destroy me completely, and I fear that I should forfeit all sense of duty out of love for you.

Ensure that you have my heart, always and forever. Regardless of what the future holds for us, no matter where we may go away from each other, part of me will forever remain with you.

Forever yours, James

P.S. The child that you carry needs to be born in love, not in shame. If you cannot bear to keep this secret from anyone, then I comprehend. But be aware of this, I would take our child in a heartbeat if things were otherwise.

Emma sat back on her heels, her hands trembling around the letter. A child? Her grandmother had a child with James before she married Emma's grandfather?

She quickly rummaged through the rest of the box and found a black and white photograph. It was of a good-looking young man in a military uniform standing beside a beautiful young woman who was much like Emma's grandmother. On the back was written: "James and Lillian, Boston Harbor, May 1952."

Lillian. Not Elizabeth.

Emma's head spun. Who was Elizabeth, and what had become of the child in the letter? Was there a relationship somewhere she had never known about? Or was the child Catherine, Emma's mother, whose daughter Frank Matthews had informed her all these years that she was? The slamming of the front door below snapped Emma from her trance.

"Emma? Are you still up there?"

Her mother's scream echoed up from the sidewalk.

Emma quickly jammed the letters back into their box, her heart racing. "Coming, Mom," she yelled, shoving the box into her tote. Whatever was inside this box, she knew they'd turn everything her family believed about their past upside down.

Part Two: Unraveling Threads

Catherine Matthews was a no-nonsense woman. She'd weathered the storms of life with stoic fortitude at sixty-five—a miserable marriage, a premature widowhood, raising Emma alone after her husband passed away. Her salt-and-pepper bob was coiffed the same as she'd been wearing it for years, and her piercing gray eyes saw very little.

"You look like you've seen a ghost," Catherine said as Emma came down the attic stairs, her t-shirt and jeans dusted.

Emma smiled fake. "Just tired. There's so much stuff up there."

They had lunch in the kitchen where Catherine had laid out sandwiches from the deli and two bottles of sparkling water. While they ate, Emma's mind kept returning to the letters and the photo hidden in her purse. Her mother was sitting across from her with no clue that her entire back story might be a lie.

"Mom," Emma said finally, setting her sandwich down. "Did Grandma ever mention anyone by the name of Elizabeth? Or James?"

Catherine frowned, a tiny crease between her eyebrows. "James? No, I don't think so. But Elizabeth. That was Grandma's middle name. Lillian Elizabeth Caldwell, before she married your grandfather. Why do you ask?"

Emma hesitated. "I was reading some old letters in the attic. I just wondered."

Catherine nodded, seeming to believe the explanation on its face, but Emma saw a flicker of something—recognition, concern?—in her eyes.

"Your grandmother was a terribly secretive woman concerning her life before she met your grandfather," Catherine said after a moment. "I do know that she was from Boston by birth, and that she immigrated to Rhode Island just in time for my birth. Apart from that." She shrugged. "She simply never did appear to wish to talk about it."

Later that evening, after her mother had gone to bed, Emma laid out the letters on her bed in the guest bedroom of her grandmother's home. She read them slowly, unfolding the story of a war romance between James Harrington, a British officer temporarily assigned to Boston, and Lillian Caldwell, a young woman from a decent but impoverished family.

The letters had spoken of a hot romance pursued largely in secret due to Lillian's domineering parents. James had written of stolen kisses under the clock in public squares, a romantic weekend in a seaside bungalow, vows of a future that never arrived.

The last letter—the letter to Elizabeth—had been penned only two weeks before Catherine's birthday.

Emma's hands trembled as she took her phone and dialed her mother.

"Mom? I have to ask you something. Do you happen to have your birth certificate?"

There was silence on the line. "Why do you need it?"

"Please, Mom. It matters."

Twenty minutes after Catherine had received her mother's hesitant agreement, she came back into the house, a worn manila envelope clutched in her fist. Her face was white, her lips pressed into a tight line.

"What is this really about, Emma?" she asked as they sat at the kitchen table.

Instead of replying directly, Emma slowly emptied the wooden box and laid out its contents before her mother. "I found these in Grandma's hope chest."

Catherine's hand flew to her mouth when she saw the picture of James and Lillian. "Who is this man?"

"His name was James Harrington. He was British, and I think. I think he might have been your actual father."

The birth certificate lay between them, untouched. Shaking, Catherine did finally reach out and grasp it, opening the document she'd likely not seen for decades.

Father's Name: Frank Matthews Mother's Name: Elizabeth Caldwell

"Elizabeth," Catherine whispered. "But my mother's name was Lillian."

Emma nudged the final letter over to her mother. "I think she used her middle name when she had you. To protect herself, maybe. To start over."

Catherine cried reading the letter. She looked up at Emma, tears of agony and bewilderment in her eyes, when she'd finished. "All these years, she let me believe. Frank Matthews wasn't my father? The man who brought me up, whom I mourned when he passed away—he wasn't my father?"

"He was your father in every way that matters," Emma spoke softly, extending her hand to her friend. "But I don't think we have the complete story."

Catherine wiped away her tears and stood upright, that same bravery revealing itself. "We need to know more about James Harrington. If this letter is true, I have a completely different past than I ever thought."

I bumped into them the next morning at the town library, where they were poring over online copies of the 1952 newspapers. They didn't take long to locate what they had set out for—a short item on the society page of the Boston Globe in July 1952:

"Notice of the wedding is given between Mr. James Edward Harrington, son of industrialist Lord William Harrington of London, and Lady Penelope Ashford, daughter of the Earl of Westmoreland. The marriage will be celebrated in September at Westminster Abbey."

"Lord Harrington," Catherine exclaimed. "My actual father was British nobility?"

Further searching yielded obituaries and articles that filled in more of the picture. Lord James Harrington had died in 1997, leaving his wife Lady Penelope and three children. The Harrington Steel plants had actually employed thousands of individuals during the post-war period, until the company was acquired by a German conglomerate in the early 2000s.

"I have half-brothers and sisters," Catherine thought. "Alive in England. People who don't even know that I exist."

Emma squeezed her mother's hand. "Do you want to try to find them? To contact them?"

Catherine gazed at the computer screen for several moments. "I don't know. This is just too much to digest." She faced Emma, her face tragic. "What I want to know most is why my mother—why Lillian—never told me the truth. And why did she retain these letters for so many years if she was trying to keep this secret."

"M maybe," Emma held back, "she was waiting for the right moment. Maybe keeping them was her own little way of doing homage to an era in her life she wasn't allowed to celebrate publicly."

"And my birth certificate. how she used Elizabeth instead of Lillian. Did she feel shame? Was I shameful to her?"

Emma remembered the neatly stored letters, the photo stored for so many years. "I don't think that it was something to be ashamed of, Mom. I think that it was protection—for her and for you. It could not have been easy to be an unwed mother in the 1950s."

As they left the library, the two women proceeded in silence, each with thoughts of her own regarding the disclosures of the day. The story was being told, but the most important question remained: Why would Lillian have held this secret all her life, even from her own daughter?

Part Three: Full Circle

Three days afterward, Emma was on her way to London with her mother. It had been an impulsive choice to take action on this new family tie following a final find in Lillian's hope chest—a newspaper clipping on the 100th anniversary of Harrington Steel, to be commemorated at the family home in Kent. The event was to be the coming weekend, at James's youngest son, Edward Harrington's, home.

"Are you sure about this?" Emma had asked for what was likely the tenth time since they'd boarded the plane. "We could write her a letter first, or call her."

Catherine shook her head. "You're not my age," she told him. "When you're my age, you don't wait for the right moment. If Edward Harrington is my half-brother, I need to see him. I need to check and see if he's got our father's eyes." She patted through her purse and fingered the photograph of James and Lillian she'd carried with her since she'd had it. "My eyes."

The English countryside lay before their car rental window the next day, green and rolling under a strange blue sky. They'd spent the night before in a pub village a few miles outside Harrington Estate, preparing themselves for what lay in store.

"What if they don't trust us?" Emma voiced the concern that had been nagging at her. "We have the letters, but no proof, really. No DNA test."

"And at least we'll have tried," Catherine stated, more bravely than Emma had hoped. "And to tell the truth, I'm not looking for money or glory. I just want to find out this piece of myself which has been so long concealed."

Harrington Estate was as an English country house should be—weathered stone fronts, neatly manicured gardens, and a sense of restrained wealth that attested to centuries of success. The party celebrating the anniversary was open to the public, tickets for sale to citizens and history enthusiasts alike, providing Emma and Catherine the ideal disguise for their initial operation.

They strolled by the display areas set to showcase the company's hundred years of industrial genius, Catherine holding on to Emma's arm with increasing tension as they approached the family history section. And there he was—a large photo of James Harrington in his youth, so similar to the young man in the photo but older, statesmanlike, his face etched with the weight of office.

"He has your smile," Emma whispered to her mother. "The same curve at the corners."

Catherine remained silent, her eyes fixed on the father she had never met.

"I notice you're staring at my father," a voice behind them.

They ached to see a man in his sixties, tall and imposing with dark hair streaked with silver and the same sharp gray eyes Catherine had. His name tag was Edward Harrington, Trustee.

Catherine's grip on Emma's arm was savage.

"Yes," Emma managed to say. "He must have been very special."

"He was indeed," Edward assented, his British accent neat and propriety.

"Though not without his quirks, no. Are you specifically interested in industrial history?"

It was now or never. Emma caught her mother's eye, which flashed only very slightly.

"Actually," Emma said tactfully, "we're interested in your father for a more personal reason." She dug into her purse and pulled out the photo of James and Lillian. "Do you recognize this young man?"

Edward took the picture, his expression altering from courteous curiosity to shock. "Where did you take this? This is my father when he was in the army. Before he met my mother."

Catherine steeled herself, calling on every remaining ounce of bravery. "That girl in the photograph with him is my mother, Lillian Caldwell. And James Harrington was my biological father, which we discovered through letters recently."

The shocked silence afterwards seemed to take forever. Edward Harrington looked at Catherine, his own eyes—so much like her own—spreading wide with shock and recognition.

"Perhaps," he said finally, his voice a trifle unsteady, "we could talk about this somewhere else."

Catherine informed him over tea that remained untouched as it cooled, of their discovery—the letters, the birth certificate, pieces of a puzzle hidden for over seventy years.

He heard her without speaking, his face a closed one. When she finished, he rose and walked to a cabinet, where he opened it and took out a small wooden box that made Emma's heart skip a beat. It was almost identical to the one they'd found in Lillian's hope chest.

"My dad left this to me when he passed away," Edward whispered. "He told me there was something in his life that he had never talked about to any of the family, not even my mum. He told me to open it when he died, so I did." He drew out a bundle of letters tied with ribbon—letters penned by Lillian.

"She wrote to him," Emma gasped. "All those years ago."

"Only once," Edward amended. "In 1972, after my grandfather—Lord William—had died. She wrote to inform him of Catherine, that she had treated her daughter well, that she had married a good man who had taken her name and his love. She included this with it."

He presented Catherine with a miniature photo of herself at about twenty, in cap and gown and mortarboard for graduation.

"My college graduation," Catherine painted, drawing the scene. "She sent him my graduation picture."

"By his own letter, which he left with these," Edward continued, "he wished many times to reach out to you, to reclaim you, but felt it would only disturb lives that had found their own happiness without him. His marriage to my mother was not a love match at the start, though they came to have a deep respect and love for each other in the years that followed. But he never forgot your mother. Or you."

Catherine brushed away her tears. "Did he write to her? Did she know he knew about me?"

Edward nodded. "He did write. Once. He thanked her for telling him about you, apologized for what he'd done, and vowed not to intrude on your life with unwanted disclosures. He kept his word."

"But why would my mother never have said anything?" Catherine inquired, the question that had plagued her since their discovery. "Why keep it a secret until she dies?"

Edward considered this. "Perhaps she believed she was doing what my father asked. Or perhaps she didn't want to intrude on your relationship with the man who had been your father in the only ways that ever mattered." He paused. "Or perhaps she was simply waiting for the right moment, and time got away from all of us."

They talked for hours, exchanging with one another their own lives and the man who had brought them together. Edward's Scottish brother and his French sister were summoned, and arrangements were made to meet again at some point.

As night was falling, Edward walked them to the car.

"You see," he told her, looking at Catherine with both awe and tenderness, "my father once told me I had his mother's eyes. Now I think that I not only have my mother's eyes, but that we share the same heritage."

Catherine laughed and wept. "I've lived all these years knowing who I was and where I belonged. It's strange to learn at age sixty-five that there's all this other branch to my family tree."

"Not strange," said Edward softly. "Rare. And although we can't retrieve the past, perhaps we can get something good out of what remains."

That night, at her inn, Emma found her mother at the window, looking out over the moonlit English landscape.

"Do you regret having come?" Emma whispered.

Catherine shook her head. "No. I'm sorry for all the years I wasted, for the grandparents I never had, the siblings I never grew up with. But I'm grateful too—for Frank Matthews, who became father when he didn't have to, and for my mother, who carried this alone to provide me with a secure life." She gazed at Emma. "And I thank you for being courageous enough to open that last letter. There are things that have to be locked away, but there are others that have to breathe in the light."

Emma imagined her grandmother, grasping so tightly to those letters all those years, holding onto this link to that love that destiny had withheld from her. Was she hoping that someone would turn up and find them one day? Was she guessing that Emma would turn out to be the one who would finally uncover what was real?

"You know what I always wonder about?" Emma said. "That last letter was never opened. It just sat there for seventy years, waiting. Like Grandma couldn't bear to read his final goodbye."

Catherine considered this. "Or perhaps she didn't need to. Perhaps she already knew what it would contain, and not opening it was her way of preserving that moment before everything changed—when the future still contained all things."

Emma smiled, grasping her mother's hand. "I like that better. That the last letter was not an end but a stop in a story that was going to be a continuous one no matter what."

Outside, the English countryside lay under the stars that had fallen on Lillian and James so many years before. Other lives had been lived on other continents, but here, through the medium of a letter that took decades to deliver its message, the lives were now entwined at last, completing the cycle with the finality of the story.

The End!

Emma remembered the neatly stored letters, the photo stored for so many years. "I don't think that it was something to be ashamed of, Mom. I think that it was protection—for her and for you. It could not have been easy to be an unwed mother in the 1950s."

As they left the library, the two women proceeded in silence, each with thoughts of her own regarding the disclosures of the day. The story was being told, but the most important question remained: Why would Lillian have held this secret all her life, even from her own daughter?

Part Three: Full Circle

Three days afterward, Emma was on her way to London with her mother. It had been an impulsive choice to take action on this new family tie following a final find in Lillian's hope chest—a newspaper clipping on the 100th anniversary of Harrington Steel, to be commemorated at the family home in Kent. The event was to be the coming weekend, at James's youngest son, Edward Harrington's, home.

"Are you sure about this?" Emma had asked for what was likely the tenth time since they'd boarded the plane. "We could write her a letter first, or call her."

Catherine shook her head. "You're not my age," she told him. "When you're my age, you don't wait for the right moment. If Edward Harrington is my half-brother, I need to see him. I need to check and see if he's got our father's eyes." She patted through her purse and fingered the photograph of James and Lillian she'd carried with her since she'd had it. "My eyes."

The English countryside lay before their car rental window the next day, green and rolling under a strange blue sky. They'd spent the night before in a pub village a few miles outside Harrington Estate, preparing themselves for what lay in store.

"What if they don't trust us?" Emma voiced the concern that had been nagging at her. "We have the letters, but no proof, really. No DNA test."

"And at least we'll have tried," Catherine stated, more bravely than Emma had hoped. "And to tell the truth, I'm not looking for money or glory. I just want to find out this piece of myself which has been so long concealed."

Harrington Estate was as an English country house should be—weathered stone fronts, neatly manicured gardens, and a sense of restrained wealth that attested to centuries of success. The party celebrating the anniversary was open to the public, tickets for sale to citizens and history enthusiasts alike, providing Emma and Catherine the ideal disguise for their initial operation.

They strolled by the display areas set to showcase the company's hundred years of industrial genius, Catherine holding on to Emma's arm with increasing tension as they approached the family history section. And there he was—a large photo of James Harrington in his youth, so similar to the young man in the photo but older, statesmanlike, his face etched with the weight of office.

"He has your smile," Emma whispered to her mother. "The same curve at the corners."

Catherine remained silent, her eyes fixed on the father she had never met.

"I notice you're staring at my father," a voice behind them.

They ached to see a man in his sixties, tall and imposing with dark hair streaked with silver and the same sharp gray eyes Catherine had. His name tag was Edward Harrington, Trustee.

Catherine's grip on Emma's arm was savage.

"Yes," Emma managed to say. "He must have been very special."

"He was indeed," Edward assented, his British accent neat and propriety.

"Though not without his quirks, no. Are you specifically interested in industrial history?"

It was now or never. Emma caught her mother's eye, which flashed only very slightly.

"Actually," Emma said tactfully, "we're interested in your father for a more personal reason." She dug into her purse and pulled out the photo of James and Lillian. "Do you recognize this young man?"

Edward took the picture, his expression altering from courteous curiosity to shock. "Where did you take this? This is my father when he was in the army. Before he met my mother."

Catherine steeled herself, calling on every remaining ounce of bravery. "That girl in the photograph with him is my mother, Lillian Caldwell. And James Harrington was my biological father, which we discovered through letters recently."

The shocked silence afterwards seemed to take forever. Edward Harrington looked at Catherine, his own eyes—so much like her own—spreading wide with shock and recognition.

"Perhaps," he said finally, his voice a trifle unsteady, "we could talk about this somewhere else."

Catherine informed him over tea that remained untouched as it cooled, of their discovery—the letters, the birth certificate, pieces of a puzzle hidden for over seventy years.

He heard her without speaking, his face a closed one. When she finished, he rose and walked to a cabinet, where he opened it and took out a small wooden box that made Emma's heart skip a beat. It was almost identical to the one they'd found in Lillian's hope chest.

"My dad left this to me when he passed away," Edward whispered. "He told me there was something in his life that he had never talked about to any of the family, not even my mum. He told me to open it when he died, so I did." He drew out a bundle of letters tied with ribbon—letters penned by Lillian.

"She wrote to him," Emma gasped. "All those years ago."

"Only once," Edward amended. "In 1972, after my grandfather—Lord William—had died. She wrote to inform him of Catherine, that she had treated her daughter well, that she had married a good man who had taken her name and his love. She included this with it."

He presented Catherine with a miniature photo of herself at about twenty, in cap and gown and mortarboard for graduation.

"My college graduation," Catherine painted, drawing the scene. "She sent him my graduation picture."

"By his own letter, which he left with these," Edward continued, "he wished many times to reach out to you, to reclaim you, but felt it would only disturb lives that had found their own happiness without him. His marriage to my mother was not a love match at the start, though they came to have a deep respect and love for each other in the years that followed. But he never forgot your mother. Or you."

Catherine brushed away her tears. "Did he write to her? Did she know he knew about me?"

Edward nodded. "He did write. Once. He thanked her for telling him about you, apologized for what he'd done, and vowed not to intrude on your life with unwanted disclosures. He kept his word."

"But why would my mother never have said anything?" Catherine inquired, the question that had plagued her since their discovery. "Why keep it a secret until she dies?"

Edward considered this. "Perhaps she believed she was doing what my father asked. Or perhaps she didn't want to intrude on your relationship with the man who had been your father in the only ways that ever mattered." He paused. "Or perhaps she was simply waiting for the right moment, and time got away from all of us."

They talked for hours, exchanging with one another their own lives and the man who had brought them together. Edward's Scottish brother and his French sister were summoned, and arrangements were made to meet again at some point.

As night was falling, Edward walked them to the car.

"You see," he told her, looking at Catherine with both awe and tenderness, "my father once told me I had his mother's eyes. Now I think that I not only have my mother's eyes, but that we share the same heritage."

Catherine laughed and wept. "I've lived all these years knowing who I was and where I belonged. It's strange to learn at age sixty-five that there's all this other branch to my family tree."

"Not strange," said Edward softly. "Rare. And although we can't retrieve the past, perhaps we can get something good out of what remains."

That night, at her inn, Emma found her mother at the window, looking out over the moonlit English landscape.

"Do you regret having come?" Emma whispered.

Catherine shook her head. "No. I'm sorry for all the years I wasted, for the grandparents I never had, the siblings I never grew up with. But I'm grateful too—for Frank Matthews, who became father when he didn't have to, and for my mother, who carried this alone to provide me with a secure life." She gazed at Emma. "And I thank you for being courageous enough to open that last letter. There are things that have to be locked away, but there are others that have to breathe in the light."

Emma imagined her grandmother, grasping so tightly to those letters all those years, holding onto this link to that love that destiny had withheld from her. Was she hoping that someone would turn up and find them one day? Was she guessing that Emma would turn out to be the one who would finally uncover what was real?

"You know what I always wonder about?" Emma said. "That last letter was never opened. It just sat there for seventy years, waiting. Like Grandma couldn't bear to read his final goodbye."

Catherine considered this. "Or perhaps she didn't need to. Perhaps she already knew what it would contain, and not opening it was her way of preserving that moment before everything changed—when the future still contained all things."

Emma smiled, grasping her mother's hand. "I like that better. That the last letter was not an end but a stop in a story that was going to be a continuous one no matter what."

Outside, the English countryside lay under the stars that had fallen on Lillian and James so many years before. Other lives had been lived on other continents, but here, through the medium of a letter that took decades to deliver its message, the lives were now entwined at last, completing the cycle with the finality of the story.
